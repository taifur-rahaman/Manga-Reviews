## Overview

*It Turns Out I've Been Invincible for a Long Time* follows Ye Changqing, who spent five years journeying around the world believing he had no cultivation talent whatsoever. Rather than chasing glory, he settled near a sect in a small town and opened a humble store. What he didn't know — and what everyone else around him could clearly see — is that his store had quietly become the holy land of all righteous individuals, the dog he casually raised is the feared demon king, and Ye Changqing himself is regarded by everyone as a Supreme Master.

## Story & World

The story is set in a classic Xianxia cultivation world full of sects, demons, and immortals. The comedy and appeal come entirely from the gap between Ye Changqing's blissfully unaware self-perception as an ordinary merchant and the reality that every being around him is in awe or terror of his unknowing supremacy. What makes this work better than the average "oblivious OP MC" manhua is the *business* angle — Ye Changqing isn't just bumbling around, he's running a shop, buying and selling, interacting with the world as a genuine civilian. That framing gives the misunderstandings more texture and keeps each chapter feeling grounded.

The plot itself is razor-thin. There's no grand overarching narrative, no real stakes, no long-term goal. At 76 chapters in, you're essentially watching the same joke get remixed over and over: Ye Changqing does something mundane, the cultivators around him interpret it as an act of supreme power, chaos or reverence ensues. It works because the execution is solid, not because the writing is deep.

## Characters

**Ye Changqing (Protagonist)** — A would-be cultivator who gave up on power and opened a small store. Completely unaware that he is the strongest person in the world. His character is static by design — the humor lives in the gap between his self-image and his actual standing — but he's written with enough warmth and contentment to be genuinely likable rather than just a vehicle for the joke.

**Supporting Cast** — A rotating ensemble of sect elders, demonic beasts masquerading as ordinary animals, and visiting cultivators who all react with terror or reverence to Ye Changqing's every mundane action. They're one-note by nature, but the variety of reactions keeps the formula fresh.

## Art & Presentation

*Rating: 7.5/10*

Full-color manhua in the standard vertical scrolling webtoon format. The art is bright, clean, and does a solid job of selling the comedic beats — facial expressions on the side characters (wide-eyed terror, barely-restrained reverence, internal screaming) are where the visual storytelling shines most. Nothing groundbreaking, but entirely competent and well-suited to the genre.

## Themes & Impact

- **Oblivious Overpowered MC**: The classic gap between self-perception and reality played for maximum comedy
- **Slice of Life Meets Power Fantasy**: Business building and daily life concealing absurd power levels
- **Accidental Influence**: How an ordinary person unknowingly shapes the world around them
- **Contentment as Strength**: Ye Changqing's complete lack of ambition is played as genuine serenity — he's happier than everyone desperately clawing for power

## Verdict

*(Interim verdict at Ch. 76 — to be revised upon completion)*

Look, this isn't a complicated series. It doesn't want to be. The entire premise is "what if the most powerful man in the world thought he was just a shopkeeper" — and it squeezes every possible drop of comedy out of that concept. Ye Changqing's complete absence of self-awareness is charming precisely because he's not dim; he's just humble and content. The story has no interest in grand narratives or character arcs, and that's fine because it never pretended otherwise.

At 76 chapters in, the formula is holding up well. The shop setting stops it from feeling like a pure rehash of *Am I Invincible* — there's more slice-of-life texture here to anchor the jokes and give the world a sense of routine. That said, 76 chapters is still early days. Whether the series can sustain this over hundreds of chapters without the joke going stale is the real question. For now though? It's good fun. Easy, reliable weekly reading material.

If you want something to unwind with — no drama, no tragedy, no grinding power-up arcs — this fits the bill perfectly.
